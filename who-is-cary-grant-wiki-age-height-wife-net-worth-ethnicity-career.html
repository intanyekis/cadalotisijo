Biography of Cary Grant

Cary Grant, full name Archibald Alec Leach, was born on January 18, 1904 in Horfield, Bristol, England, to Elias James Leach and Elsie Maria Leach. His father worked in a clothing factory ironing clothes, and his mother made a living by sewing. He had an older brother, but he died of tuberculous meningitis less than a year after his birth.

His father was an alcoholic and his mother suffered from clinical depression, so he had a difficult childhood. His father sent his mother to Glenside Hospital, a mental institution when he was nine years old. He later informed him of his mother’s death. Grant and his father moved into Grant’s grandmother’s house in Bristol.

His father remarried when he was only ten years old. It wasn’t until he was 31 years old that Cary found out that his mother was alive. His father told him this only a few days before his death. He took the necessary steps to get his mother out of the hospital in June 1935, after learning of her existence.

In terms of education and career, he started working in the theater around 1914. Jesse Lasky, then a Broadway producer, was attracted by his performance at Berlin’s Wintergarden Theater. Cary Grant received a scholarship to Fairfield Grammar School in Bristol in 1915.

Even then, he worked backstage at theaters in Bristol in the evenings. Due to his behavior, he was expelled from school on March 13, 1918. After his release, he rejoined “Bob Pender’s Stage Troupe,” with whom he had previously trained as a stilt walker and toured.

Career

Cary Grant made his debut in the comedy “To-night” (1932), playing an Olympic javelin thrower. Despite his aversion to the role, his performance in the film was praised by critics. He then appeared as a playboy in several films, including “Blue Venus,” “Happily to Hell,” and “The Devil and the Deep,” all of which were released in 1932.

His film “She Done Him Wrong”, which was released in 1933, did well financially and critically. He was nominated for an Academy Award for his performance in “When You’re in Love” in 1937. In the same year, he starred in the comedy “Topper”, which was his first major comedy success. In 1940, he appeared in the successful comedy “My Favorite Woman”.

He received his first Oscar nomination for his performance in the 1941 drama Penny Serenade. Two years later, in 1943, he starred in “Destination Tokyo”, a war film that is considered one of his best performances of all time. He received his second Oscar nomination in 1944 for his role in the film “None but the Lonely Heart”. His performance in 1947’s “The Bachelor and the Bobby-Soxer” also earned him much critical acclaim.

In 1949, he starred in the comedy I Was a Male War Bride, which became 20th Century Fox’s highest-grossing film of the year. By this time he had established himself as one of the highest paid actors in the industry. The last film he worked on was the comedy “Walk, don’t run” from 1966. Besides being an excellent actor, he also worked in real estate. He was a member of the boards of directors of Fabergé, MGM Grand Hotel, Hollywood Park, Academy of Magical Arts and Western Airlines.
